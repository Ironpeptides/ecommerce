The Biology of Aging: What Research Has Established

In 2013, a landmark paper published in Cell by López-Otín and colleagues proposed a framework that has since become foundational to aging biology: the Hallmarks of Aging. These nine interconnected biological processes — including telomere attrition, loss of proteostasis, deregulated nutrient sensing, and mitochondrial dysfunction — were proposed as the mechanistic drivers of organismal aging.

What makes this framework important for understanding the peptide research covered in this article is that NAD+, Epithalon, and MOTS-C each map precisely onto different hallmarks — and emerging research suggests the hallmarks themselves are interconnected in ways that make multi-pathway interventions scientifically compelling.

Understanding these three compounds requires understanding that aging is not a single process. It is a cascade of biological failures, each accelerating others. Telomere shortening disrupts gene expression. Declining NAD+ impairs the sirtuins that regulate DNA repair. Reduced mitochondrial signaling compromises metabolic efficiency. The hallmarks loop back on each other — which is precisely why researchers are interested in compounds that target different nodes simultaneously.

"Aging is not one thing. It is a cascade — each biological failure accelerating others. The research question is no longer whether these pathways exist. It is whether we can study what happens when we target multiple nodes at once."

This article examines the three compounds that have attracted the most sustained scientific attention in longevity biology — what the peer-reviewed literature says about their mechanisms, what the study findings actually show, and what the meaningful limitations of the current evidence base are.

NAD+ — The Cellular Fuel Regulator

Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide is not a peptide in the traditional sense — it is a coenzyme found in every living cell on earth, first identified in 1906 by biochemist Sir Arthur Harden from yeast extract. For most of the twentieth century, NAD+ was understood primarily as a metabolic cofactor — a molecule that shuttles electrons during energy production. That understanding was not wrong. But it was dramatically incomplete.

The modern understanding of NAD+ biology — and why it became central to longevity research — emerged from discoveries in the late 1990s and early 2000s. Researchers found that NAD+ serves as the essential substrate for a class of proteins called sirtuins. Sirtuins regulate gene expression, DNA repair, inflammation, and metabolic homeostasis. Without adequate NAD+, sirtuins cannot function. And NAD+, it turns out, declines substantially with age.

The Age-Related Decline

A 2026 expert review published in Nature Aging — authored by more than 25 international scientists — consolidated decades of research on NAD+ and aging. The review confirmed that NAD+ levels decline across multiple tissues with age — including skin, blood, liver, skeletal muscle, and brain tissue. This decline is not trivial. In some tissues, NAD+ concentrations in older organisms are less than half what they are in young ones.

Mechanisms: Why NAD+ Matters for Aging Research

Primary Mechanisms in Research Literature

Sirtuin Activation: Sirtuins (SIRT1–7) are NAD+-dependent deacylases that regulate chromatin structure, gene expression, mitochondrial biogenesis, and inflammatory signalling. SIRT1 in particular has been extensively studied for its role in DNA damage response and metabolic regulation. NAD+ is consumed in every sirtuin-catalysed reaction — meaning sirtuin activity is directly rate-limited by NAD+ availability.

PARP-Mediated DNA Repair: Poly(ADP-ribose) polymerases — PARPs — are the primary cellular responders to DNA strand breaks. They consume NAD+ at high rates during DNA repair. As organisms age and accumulate more DNA damage, PARP activity increases, depleting NAD+ further — creating a feedback loop that accelerates both aging phenotypes simultaneously.

Mitochondrial Function: NAD+ and its reduced form NADH are essential to the electron transport chain — the process by which cells generate ATP. Declining NAD+ is associated with reduced mitochondrial efficiency, which compounds the metabolic decline observed in aging tissue. This links NAD+ directly to the mitochondrial dysfunction hallmark of aging.

The Neurodegeneration Research Connection

Among the most compelling recent findings in NAD+ research is its emerging connection to neurodegeneration. A 2025 human clinical study in 46 older adults with mild cognitive impairment found that NAD+ precursor supplementation produced a 7% reduction in phosphorylated tau — the protein aggregate strongly associated with Alzheimer's disease pathology. These are early findings, and the study size is small. But they represent the first human data connecting NAD+ restoration to a measurable change in a neurodegeneration biomarker, and they have driven significant follow-up investigation.

Researchers from the University of Oslo and Akershus University Hospital, contributing to the Nature Aging review, emphasised that NAD+ represents one of the most mechanistically credible targets in the biology of both healthspan and lifespan — with a clinical trial pipeline spanning cardiovascular aging, skeletal muscle function, cognitive decline, and metabolic disease.

Research Considerations

The evidence base for NAD+ is unusually strong at the mechanistic level — the sirtuin and PARP pathways are well-characterised and replicated across many laboratory systems. The human clinical data is still developing. Most randomised controlled trials use NAD+ precursors (NMN or NR) rather than NAD+ itself, because direct supplementation faces absorption challenges. The most meaningful human data is 1–3 years old at most, and long-term trial data in healthy humans does not yet exist at scale. Researchers in this space acknowledge this explicitly.

Epithalon — The Telomere Peptide

Epithalon (also spelled Epitalon in the Russian literature, where the bulk of the research originates) is a synthetic tetrapeptide — just four amino acids in sequence: Alanine-Glutamic acid-Aspartic acid-Glycine (Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly). It was developed by Dr. Vladimir Khavinson at the St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology, derived from research on polypeptide extracts of the bovine pineal gland in the 1980s.

What makes Epithalon scientifically remarkable is a combination of factors that rarely coincide: an unusually well-defined mechanism, an unusually extensive research program spanning four decades, and a depth of published literature that is extraordinary for a compound that has never entered Western regulatory processes.

The Telomere Connection

Telomeres are the repetitive DNA sequences — TTAGGG — that cap the ends of chromosomes and protect them from degradation. Every time a somatic cell divides, its telomeres shorten slightly. When telomere length falls below a critical threshold, the cell enters senescence or apoptosis — it stops dividing. This progressive telomere attrition is a recognised hallmark of biological aging. Cells with critically short telomeres contribute to the tissue dysfunction and inflammatory signalling patterns observed in aged organisms.

Telomerase is the enzyme that rebuilds telomeres. In most adult somatic cells, telomerase expression is suppressed — which is why telomere attrition accumulates across a lifetime. What Khavinson's team published in the late 1990s was striking: Epithalon activates telomerase in human somatic fetal cells in vitro, extending their replicative lifespan by approximately 42% versus controls.

Pineal Gland Regulation

Epithalon's second major mechanism involves the pineal gland and melatonin. The pineal gland's melatonin output declines significantly with age — a change associated with disrupted circadian rhythms, impaired sleep architecture, and reduced antioxidant activity. Epithalon appears to restore melatonin secretion in aging organisms. In aged primate studies, Epithalon administration restored melatonin production to levels more characteristic of younger animals.

This melatonin-restoration mechanism is biologically significant because melatonin itself has established antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties that are relevant to aging. The pineal pathway represents a distinct mechanistic thread from telomere biology — suggesting Epithalon may operate through two independent but complementary aging-relevant processes simultaneously.

Dual Mechanism Summary

Pathway 1 — Telomerase Activation: Epithalon upregulates hTERT (human telomerase reverse transcriptase), the catalytic subunit of the telomerase complex, in somatic cells. This is the most extensively replicated finding in the Epithalon literature and is the primary basis for its classification as a longevity research compound.

Pathway 2 — Pineal/Melatonin Restoration: Epithalon appears to regulate the pineal gland's output of melatonin in aging organisms, restoring the age-related decline in nocturnal melatonin secretion. Animal studies across rodent and primate models have demonstrated this effect consistently.

Animal Lifespan Studies

Beyond the in vitro telomerase findings, Khavinson's program produced extensive animal longevity data. Studies in mice, rats, and rhesus monkeys showed consistent patterns: Epithalon-treated animals demonstrated extended median and maximum lifespan in multiple rodent strains, reduced tumour incidence in tumour-prone models, and improved markers of metabolic and cardiovascular function in aged animals. The primate data is particularly noteworthy because of the closer biological proximity to human physiology.

Research Considerations

The critical limitation acknowledged by researchers in this space is the concentration of the published literature in Russian-language journals and within Khavinson's own research group. Independent replication by Western institutions is limited. The in vitro telomerase activation finding is well-replicated within the body of work, but large-scale, randomised, placebo-controlled human trials of the kind that would satisfy Western regulatory standards do not exist for Epithalon. The 12-year cohort data is observational, not controlled. Researchers approaching this compound should weigh the mechanistic plausibility — which is high — against the relative thinness of the independent evidence base compared to more mainstream longevity compounds.

MOTS-C — The Mitochondrial Exercise Signal

MOTS-C is the newest of the three compounds discussed here — first described in a 2015 publication from researchers at the University of Southern California — and arguably the most conceptually surprising. Not because of what it does, but because of where it comes from.

Almost all biologically active peptides produced by the human body are encoded in nuclear DNA. MOTS-C is one of a small, recently discovered class of exceptions: it is encoded in mitochondrial DNA. Specifically, it is encoded within the 12S ribosomal RNA gene of the mitochondrial genome — a region previously assumed to be non-coding for peptides. Its discovery fundamentally revised understanding of what the mitochondrial genome does.

The Bi-Genomic Signalling Loop

What MOTS-C does after being produced is equally remarkable. Under conditions of metabolic stress or exercise, MOTS-C translocates from the mitochondria to the cell nucleus, where it directly regulates nuclear gene expression. This creates what USC researchers described as a "bi-genomic signalling loop" — a communication pathway between the mitochondrial genome and the nuclear genome that had not been previously characterised.

A 2021 paper in Nature Communications by Lee et al. — arguably the most significant publication in MOTS-C research to date — described MOTS-C as "a mitochondrial-encoded exercise-induced regulator of age-dependent physical decline and muscle homeostasis." The paper documented that human exercise increases circulating MOTS-C levels in skeletal muscle and blood, and that exogenously administered MOTS-C in aged mice improved physical capacity, grip strength, and metabolic markers.

AMPK: The Mechanism

MOTS-C's primary molecular target is AMPK — AMP-activated protein kinase — often described as the master switch for cellular energy homeostasis. When cellular energy is low (AMP:ATP ratio rises), AMPK is activated. Once active, it promotes glucose uptake into cells, stimulates fatty acid oxidation, drives mitochondrial biogenesis, and inhibits energy-consuming anabolic processes.

AMPK activation is also one of the primary mechanisms through which exercise produces its metabolic benefits. This is the basis for MOTS-C's classification in research literature as an exercise mimetic — a compound that activates some of the same molecular pathways as physical exercise without requiring the physical activity itself.

MOTS-C Mechanism Chain

Step 1: Cellular metabolic stress or exercise triggers MOTS-C expression in mitochondria. MOTS-C is synthesised from the 12S rRNA gene of the mitochondrial genome.

Step 2: MOTS-C translocates to the cell nucleus, where it regulates nuclear gene expression — including genes involved in heat shock response, glucose metabolism, and mitochondrial function.

Step 3: MOTS-C activates AMPK, triggering downstream effects: increased glucose uptake, enhanced fatty acid oxidation, mitochondrial biogenesis, and suppression of inflammatory signalling.

Step 4: Systemic MOTS-C circulates in blood — levels measurable and age-dependent — functioning as an endocrine-like signal communicating mitochondrial status to distant tissues.

The Age-Related Decline

One of the most compelling research observations around MOTS-C is its age-dependence. Circulating MOTS-C levels in older adults are approximately 21% lower than in younger counterparts. This decline correlates with the metabolic changes that characterise aging — reduced insulin sensitivity, decreased exercise tolerance, impaired mitochondrial function. Researchers are studying whether the MOTS-C decline is a marker of metabolic aging, a driver of it, or both.

Research Considerations

MOTS-C is the youngest compound in this discussion — first described in 2015 — and its research base, while rapidly growing, is significantly less extensive than NAD+ or Epithalon. The majority of mechanistic and efficacy data comes from preclinical models. Human data is limited. The Nature Communications paper represents the best evidence to date, and it involves animal models with human correlational data (exercise-induced MOTS-C increases in humans), not controlled human trials. Researchers approaching this compound should understand it as a highly mechanistically promising target with a strong preclinical and correlational evidence base, and a human clinical trial base that is still early-stage.

Where These Three Pathways Converge

The scientific interest in studying NAD+, Epithalon, and MOTS-C together — rather than in isolation — stems from the observation that their mechanisms are not merely complementary. They are functionally interdependent at multiple nodes.

"The convergence of telomere biology, NAD+ metabolism, and mitochondrial signalling represents one of the most scientifically credible frontiers in longevity research — because these are not three separate problems. They are three faces of the same cascade."

The NAD+–Mitochondria Connection

NAD+ and MOTS-C operate within closely related systems. NAD+ is essential for the electron transport chain — the process that takes place inside mitochondria to generate ATP. MOTS-C is encoded by mitochondrial DNA and promotes mitochondrial biogenesis via AMPK. Both declining NAD+ and declining MOTS-C impair mitochondrial function — the third hallmark of aging. Restoring either compound, in preclinical models, partially rescues mitochondrial phenotypes. Studying them together addresses the same hallmark through two independent mechanisms.

The Sirtuin–Telomere Link

SIRT6, one of the NAD+-dependent sirtuins, has established roles in telomere maintenance — it deacetylates histones at telomere regions and is required for proper telomere replication. NAD+ depletion impairs SIRT6 function, which contributes to telomere instability — the same problem Epithalon addresses through a different route (direct telomerase activation). This creates a mechanistic rationale for studying the two compounds as addressing complementary aspects of the same underlying problem.

Research Context and Limitations

The longevity peptide research field is marked by a consistent pattern: mechanistic evidence that is sophisticated, compelling, and well-replicated — alongside human clinical data that lags significantly behind. This is not unique to these three compounds. It reflects the broader challenge of longevity research, where the primary outcome of interest (extended healthspan or lifespan in humans) requires decades to measure, and regulatory frameworks were not designed for preventive aging interventions.

What the Evidence Actually Supports

For NAD+: the mechanistic evidence is strong, the preclinical evidence is extensive, and the human trial data is growing — with early signals on metabolic and cognitive biomarkers. The compound has the most mature human evidence base of the three discussed here.

For Epithalon: the telomerase activation finding in human cells in vitro is well-replicated within the Khavinson literature. The animal longevity data is extensive. The limitation is the concentration of research in one group and the absence of large-scale Western RCTs. Researchers should engage with this evidence base critically, acknowledging both its mechanistic credibility and its replication limitations.

For MOTS-C: this is the youngest and most rapidly developing area. The mechanistic novelty is genuine — a mitochondrially-encoded peptide that communicates with the nucleus is a real and significant biological discovery. The preclinical data is compelling. Human data is early-stage. Researchers approaching MOTS-C should expect the evidence base to develop substantially in the next five years as clinical trials in this space begin to report.

The Combinatorial Research Question

No published clinical trial has yet examined NAD+, Epithalon, and MOTS-C in combination in human subjects. The rationale for studying them together is mechanistically grounded — they address different hallmarks of aging through complementary pathways — but combinatorial evidence in humans does not yet exist. Research groups studying longevity interventions have begun designing trials that combine NAD+ restoration with other compounds targeting different hallmarks, and this approach is gaining traction in the field. The MOTS-C and Epithalon evidence bases are expected to develop significantly as interest in the longevity peptide research space accelerates.

Conclusion

The biology of aging is not a single-pathway problem. Decades of research — from the Hallmarks of Aging framework to the specific molecular evidence reviewed here — make clear that the cellular cascade driving organismal aging involves multiple intersecting systems. Telomere shortening, NAD+ depletion, and mitochondrial dysfunction do not occur in isolation. They accelerate each other.

NAD+, Epithalon, and MOTS-C represent three of the most mechanistically well-characterised compounds in longevity research, each addressing a different node in that cascade. NAD+'s role in sirtuin activation and DNA repair is supported by some of the most sophisticated mechanistic data in all of biology, and its human clinical evidence is growing. Epithalon's telomerase-activating properties, while the product of a research program concentrated in one institution, are mechanistically plausible and supported by an unusually extensive body of published work. MOTS-C's discovery — that the mitochondrial genome produces peptides that regulate nuclear gene expression — is a genuinely novel finding in biology, with implications still being characterised.

The research question driving current longevity science is whether combining compounds that target different aging hallmarks produces effects greater than targeting any single pathway. That question does not yet have a definitive answer in human subjects. But it is the right question — and the evidence reviewed here explains why these three compounds, specifically, are at the centre of it.
